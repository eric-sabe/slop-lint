## History of Coffee

Coffee is one of the world’s most widely consumed beverages, with a history that spans centuries and continents. Its origins are traditionally traced to the highlands of Ethiopia, where the coffee plant, *Coffea*, grows wild. According to a popular legend, a goat herder named Kaldi noticed that his goats became unusually energetic after eating the red berries of a certain shrub. While the story is difficult to verify, it reflects coffee’s deep association with Ethiopia, where coffee has long been consumed in various forms, including as a food, a medicinal preparation, and a ceremonial drink.

The earliest reliable evidence of coffee drinking comes from the Arabian Peninsula, particularly Yemen, in the 15th century. Sufi communities in Yemen used coffee to stay awake during nighttime religious devotions. Coffee was prepared by roasting and brewing the seeds—commonly called beans—of the coffee cherry, producing a stimulating beverage valued for its ability to increase alertness. From Yemen, coffee spread to the holy cities of Mecca and Medina, where it became popular among pilgrims and scholars.

By the 16th century, coffee had spread throughout the Ottoman Empire, including Cairo, Damascus, Baghdad, and Istanbul. Coffeehouses, known in the Middle East as qahveh khaneh, became important social institutions. They were places where men gathered to drink coffee, discuss politics, listen to music, play games, and exchange news. Because of their role in public debate, coffeehouses sometimes attracted suspicion from political and religious authorities. At various times, rulers attempted to ban coffee or close coffeehouses, claiming they encouraged idleness or dissent. These bans generally failed, and coffee continued to grow in popularity.

Coffee entered Europe in the 16th and 17th centuries through trade with the Ottoman Empire and the Mediterranean world. Venice, a major commercial center, was among the first European cities to import coffee. At first, some Europeans regarded it with suspicion, calling it a “Muslim drink,” but its popularity increased rapidly. By the mid-17th century, coffeehouses had appeared in major European cities, including London, Paris, Vienna, and Amsterdam. These establishments became centers of intellectual and commercial activity. In England, coffeehouses were nicknamed “penny universities” because, for the price of a cup of coffee, customers could engage in conversation, read newspapers, and hear lectures or debates.

European demand for coffee led to efforts to cultivate the plant outside its original growing regions. For much of coffee’s early commercial history, Yemen controlled production and export, carefully guarding fertile beans to prevent cultivation elsewhere. However, by the late 17th century, the Dutch had succeeded in obtaining coffee plants and cultivating them in their colonies, especially on the island of Java in present-day Indonesia. Java became so closely associated with coffee that the word “java” later became a common synonym for the beverage.

In the 18th century, coffee cultivation expanded across European colonial territories. The French introduced coffee to the Caribbean, particularly Martinique, from which it spread to other islands. Coffee plantations were established in Saint-Domingue (modern Haiti), Jamaica, Cuba, and elsewhere. The Portuguese introduced coffee to Brazil in the early 18th century. Over time, Brazil became the world’s largest coffee producer, a position it has held for much of modern history.

The expansion of coffee production was closely tied to colonialism and slavery. In the Caribbean and Latin America, coffee plantations relied heavily on enslaved African labor and later on other forms of coerced or poorly paid labor. In Brazil, coffee production contributed to the growth of large estates and shaped the country’s economy, politics, and social structure. Even after the abolition of slavery, coffee cultivation often depended on exploitative labor systems, including debt peonage and low-wage migrant labor.

During the 19th century, coffee became a truly global commodity. Improvements in transportation, including steamships and railways, made it easier to move coffee from plantations to ports and then to markets around the world. Industrialization and urbanization increased demand, as coffee became associated with work, productivity, and modern city life. In the United States, coffee gained particular popularity after the American Revolution, partly because tea had become politically associated with British rule. By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, innovations such as vacuum packaging, instant coffee, and large-scale roasting helped make coffee a household staple.

The 20th century saw both the mass production and cultural diversification of coffee. Instant coffee became important during the world wars because it was easy to transport and prepare for soldiers. After World War II, commercial coffee brands expanded rapidly, especially in North America and Europe. At the same time, distinct coffee cultures flourished around the world: espresso bars in Italy, café culture in France, strong boiled coffee traditions in Scandinavia and the Middle East, and sweetened coffee drinks in many parts of Latin America and Asia.

From the late 20th century onward, a “specialty coffee” movement transformed the industry. Consumers became more interested in the origin, processing, roasting, and brewing of coffee. Independent cafés and roasters promoted single-origin beans, lighter roasts, and manual brewing methods. Concerns about fair trade, environmental sustainability, and the livelihoods of coffee farmers also became more prominent.

Today, coffee is grown in more than 70 countries, primarily in the “coffee belt” between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn. The two most commercially important species are *Coffea arabica*, prized for its complex flavor, and *Coffea canephora*, commonly known as robusta, valued for its higher caffeine content and resilience. Coffee remains a major global commodity and a daily ritual for billions of people, linking ancient traditions, colonial histories, modern commerce, and contemporary café culture.
