The human immune system is the body’s defense network against harmful microbes, such as bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites, as well as abnormal cells, including cancer cells. It is not a single organ, but a coordinated system of cells, tissues, organs, and chemical signals that constantly monitor the body, recognize danger, attack threats, and remember past infections.

A useful way to understand the immune system is to divide it into two main parts: the innate immune system and the adaptive immune system.

The **innate immune system** is the body’s first line of defense. It responds quickly, often within minutes or hours, and reacts in a general way to many kinds of threats. Physical barriers are part of innate immunity. The skin blocks many microbes from entering, while mucus in the nose, throat, lungs, and digestive tract traps invaders. Tears, saliva, stomach acid, and enzymes also help destroy microbes before they can cause disease.

If pathogens get past these barriers, innate immune cells respond. **Phagocytes**, such as neutrophils and macrophages, engulf and digest microbes. Macrophages also help alert the rest of the immune system by releasing chemical signals called cytokines. **Natural killer cells** patrol the body and destroy cells that appear infected by viruses or have become cancerous. Another part of innate immunity is the **complement system**, a group of proteins in the blood that can mark microbes for destruction, attract immune cells, or punch holes in bacterial membranes.

Inflammation is one of the key responses of innate immunity. When tissue is injured or infected, immune cells release signals that increase blood flow and make blood vessels more permeable. This allows immune cells and proteins to move into the affected tissue. The familiar signs of inflammation—redness, heat, swelling, pain, and sometimes loss of function—are caused by this increased immune activity. Although inflammation can be uncomfortable, it is often necessary for healing and infection control.

The **adaptive immune system** is slower to respond the first time it encounters a specific pathogen, but it is highly targeted and has memory. This means it can respond much faster and more strongly if the same pathogen appears again. The main cells of adaptive immunity are **B cells** and **T cells**, both types of lymphocytes.

B cells are responsible for producing **antibodies**. Antibodies are Y-shaped proteins that bind to specific molecules, called antigens, on the surface of pathogens or toxins. Once antibodies attach to a target, they can neutralize it directly, block viruses from entering cells, or mark microbes so other immune cells can destroy them. Some B cells become **plasma cells**, which produce large amounts of antibody. Others become **memory B cells**, which remain in the body for years or even decades.

T cells have several roles. **Helper T cells** coordinate immune responses by releasing cytokines that activate B cells, macrophages, and other T cells. **Cytotoxic T cells**, also called killer T cells, destroy the body’s own cells when those cells are infected by viruses or have become abnormal. They do this by recognizing small fragments of proteins displayed on the surface of cells. These displayed fragments act like identification tags, showing whether a cell is healthy or infected.

The immune system depends heavily on communication. Cells send chemical messages to one another using cytokines and other signaling molecules. These signals can increase immune activity, attract cells to an infection site, stimulate fever, or calm the response once the danger has passed. Fever itself can be helpful because higher body temperature can slow the growth of some microbes and improve certain immune functions.

Several organs and tissues support immunity. The **bone marrow** produces blood cells, including many immune cells. The **thymus** is where T cells mature and learn to distinguish the body’s own tissues from foreign targets. **Lymph nodes** act like filtering stations, where immune cells encounter antigens and begin adaptive responses. The **spleen** filters blood and helps detect blood-borne infections. Mucosal tissues in the gut, airways, and other surfaces also contain large numbers of immune cells because these areas are common entry points for microbes.

A major feature of the immune system is its ability to distinguish “self” from “non-self.” This prevents it from attacking the body’s own tissues. During development, many immune cells that strongly react against self molecules are eliminated or inactivated. Other regulatory mechanisms keep immune responses under control. When these systems fail, **autoimmune diseases** can occur, such as type 1 diabetes, lupus, or rheumatoid arthritis, in which the immune system mistakenly attacks healthy tissue.

The immune system can also overreact to harmless substances. This is what happens in **allergies**, where the immune system responds to things like pollen, dust mites, or certain foods as though they are dangerous. In severe cases, allergic reactions can cause anaphylaxis, a life-threatening whole-body reaction.

Vaccines work by using the adaptive immune system’s memory. A vaccine exposes the body to a harmless form or piece of a pathogen, prompting B cells and T cells to create memory cells. Later, if the real pathogen appears, the immune system can respond quickly, often preventing illness or making it much milder.

In short, the immune system is a layered defense system. It blocks invaders, detects danger, attacks infections, removes damaged cells, remembers previous threats, and regulates itself to avoid unnecessary harm. Its strength comes from coordination: many different cells and signals working together to protect the body while preserving its own healthy tissues.
