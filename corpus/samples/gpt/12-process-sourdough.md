Baking sourdough bread from scratch is a slow but rewarding process. Unlike bread made with commercial yeast, sourdough rises through a living culture of wild yeast and beneficial bacteria called a starter. The process takes patience, but the ingredients are simple: flour, water, and salt.

## 1. Make a sourdough starter

A sourdough starter is a mixture of flour and water that captures wild yeast from the environment and flour itself.

**You will need:**
- Whole wheat or rye flour, plus all-purpose or bread flour
- Water, preferably filtered or dechlorinated
- A clean jar
- A spoon or spatula

**Day 1:** Mix 50 grams flour with 50 grams water in a jar. Stir until no dry flour remains. Cover loosely with a lid, cloth, or plastic wrap. Leave at room temperature.

**Days 2–7:** Each day, discard about half the starter and feed it with 50 grams flour and 50 grams water. Stir well and cover loosely again.

After a few days, you should see bubbles and notice a tangy smell. By about day 5 to 7, the starter should rise after feeding and then fall back down. It is ready to bake with when it doubles in size within 4–6 hours of feeding and smells pleasantly sour, fruity, or yeasty.

## 2. Gather bread ingredients

For one basic loaf, use:

- 100 grams active sourdough starter
- 375 grams water
- 500 grams bread flour
- 10 grams salt

Bread flour gives a stronger structure, but all-purpose flour can also work. A kitchen scale is strongly recommended because sourdough depends on accurate ratios.

## 3. Mix the dough

In a large bowl, combine 100 grams active starter and 350 grams of the water. Stir to loosen the starter. Add 500 grams flour and mix until the flour is fully hydrated. The dough will look shaggy and sticky.

Let it rest for 30–60 minutes. This rest is called an autolyse. It helps the flour absorb water and makes the dough easier to stretch.

After the rest, dissolve 10 grams salt in the remaining 25 grams water and work it into the dough. Pinch and fold the dough until the saltwater is incorporated.

## 4. Bulk fermentation and stretch-and-folds

Bulk fermentation is the first long rise. Cover the bowl and let the dough sit at room temperature for about 4–6 hours, depending on the warmth of your kitchen and the strength of your starter.

During the first 2 hours, do a set of stretch-and-folds every 30 minutes. To do this, wet your hand, grab one side of the dough, stretch it upward, and fold it over itself. Rotate the bowl and repeat on all four sides. This builds gluten strength without kneading.

As fermentation continues, the dough should become smoother, puffier, and slightly domed. It may increase in volume by about 50–75%, though it does not always need to double. Look for bubbles around the sides and a soft, airy feel.

## 5. Shape the loaf

Lightly flour your work surface and gently turn out the dough. Try not to deflate it too much.

First, pre-shape it into a loose round by folding the edges toward the center and turning it seam-side down. Let it rest for 15–20 minutes.

Then shape the final loaf. For a round boule, flip the dough seam-side up, fold the edges into the center, then turn it seam-side down and gently drag it across the counter to create surface tension. The outside should feel taut but not torn.

Place the shaped dough seam-side up in a floured proofing basket or a bowl lined with a floured towel.

## 6. Final proof

Cover the dough and let it proof. You have two options:

**Room-temperature proof:** Let it rise for 1–3 hours, until slightly puffy.

**Cold proof:** Refrigerate it for 8–16 hours. This develops more flavor and makes the dough easier to score. Many bakers prefer this method.

The dough is ready when it slowly springs back after being gently poked. If it springs back immediately, it needs more time. If it does not spring back at all, it may be overproofed.

## 7. Bake the bread

About 45 minutes before baking, preheat your oven to 475°F / 245°C with a Dutch oven inside. The Dutch oven traps steam, which helps the loaf rise and develop a crisp crust.

Carefully turn the dough onto parchment paper. Dust off excess flour if needed. Score the top with a sharp knife or razor blade. A simple slash across the top allows the bread to expand in the oven.

Remove the hot Dutch oven carefully. Lift the dough in using the parchment. Cover with the lid and bake for 20 minutes.

Then remove the lid, reduce the oven temperature to 450°F / 230°C, and bake another 20–25 minutes, until the crust is deep golden brown. The bread should sound hollow when tapped on the bottom.

## 8. Cool before slicing

Let the loaf cool on a wire rack for at least 1 hour, preferably 2. Cutting too soon can make the crumb gummy because the inside is still setting.

Once cooled, slice and enjoy. Good sourdough has a crisp crust, chewy interior, and complex tangy flavor. If your first loaf is dense or flat, do not worry. Sourdough improves with practice, and every bake teaches you something about timing, temperature, and fermentation.
