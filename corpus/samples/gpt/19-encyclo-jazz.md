Jazz originated in the United States in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, emerging most strongly from African American communities in New Orleans. Its beginnings were not the result of a single invention or one identifiable founder, but rather the blending of many musical traditions: African rhythms, European harmony, church music, blues, ragtime, marching bands, and the everyday sounds of a diverse port city. Jazz grew out of social life—dances, parades, funerals, bars, theaters, and street performances—and from the creativity of musicians who transformed inherited materials into something new.

The deepest roots of jazz lie in the musical practices of enslaved Africans and their descendants in North America. African musical traditions emphasized rhythm, call-and-response, improvisation, communal participation, and expressive vocal techniques. Although slavery violently disrupted African cultures, many musical ideas survived and adapted. Work songs, field hollers, spirituals, and later gospel music all carried elements that would become central to jazz: flexible rhythm, personal expression, bent notes, and a strong connection between music and daily experience.

One important precursor to jazz was the blues. The blues developed in the American South after the Civil War, especially among African American communities facing poverty, segregation, and displacement. Blues songs often used a repeated harmonic pattern, commonly the twelve-bar blues, and lyrics that expressed hardship, humor, love, loss, and resilience. Musically, the blues introduced “blue notes”—pitches sung or played slightly lower or bent for expressive effect. Jazz musicians absorbed the blues deeply, not only as a form but as a feeling and vocabulary.

Another major influence was ragtime, a composed piano style that became popular in the 1890s and early 1900s. Ragtime featured syncopation, meaning accents placed in unexpected parts of the beat. Scott Joplin, the most famous ragtime composer, wrote pieces such as “Maple Leaf Rag” and “The Entertainer,” which helped bring syncopated music to a national audience. Ragtime was generally more structured and written out than early jazz, but its rhythmic drive and lively character had a major impact on jazz musicians.

New Orleans played a special role in the birth of jazz because of its unusual cultural mixture. The city had French, Spanish, African, Caribbean, Creole, and Anglo-American influences. As a port city, it was full of movement—people, goods, languages, and music. Brass bands were especially important. They performed at parades, dances, political events, and funerals. In the famous New Orleans “jazz funeral” tradition, bands might play solemn music on the way to the cemetery and joyful, swinging music on the return, celebrating life as well as mourning death.

New Orleans also had a strong tradition of public music-making. Congo Square, where enslaved and free Black people gathered in the 18th and 19th centuries to dance, drum, and sing, became a symbol of African cultural survival in the city. While jazz did not simply “begin” there, the memory and practice of African-derived rhythm and communal performance helped shape the environment from which jazz emerged.

The city’s Creole musicians also contributed significantly. Many Creoles of color had formal musical training and were familiar with European classical techniques, reading notation, and playing in orchestras. After segregation laws hardened in the late 19th century, Creole musicians increasingly performed alongside Black musicians whose styles were more rooted in blues, street music, and oral tradition. The interaction between these groups helped create a music that combined technical skill with improvisational freedom.

Improvisation became one of the defining features of jazz. Early jazz bands often played familiar melodies—marches, blues, ragtime tunes, hymns, or popular songs—but musicians would embellish, vary, and reinterpret them. A typical early New Orleans jazz ensemble might include cornet or trumpet, clarinet, trombone, piano, banjo or guitar, bass or tuba, and drums. The trumpet often carried the main melody, the clarinet wove decorative lines around it, and the trombone added sliding counter-melodies. This collective improvisation gave early jazz its energetic, conversational sound.

Many early jazz musicians performed in dance halls, saloons, riverboats, and entertainment districts. Storyville, New Orleans’s red-light district, is often mentioned in jazz history because musicians found work there, but it was only one part of the city’s musical life. In the 1910s and 1920s, jazz spread beyond New Orleans. The Great Migration carried African American musicians and communities northward to cities such as Chicago, New York, Detroit, and Kansas City. By the 1920s, often called the Jazz Age, the music had become a symbol of modern American culture. Jazz began as a music of mixture, movement, and improvisation—and those qualities have continued to define it ever since.
