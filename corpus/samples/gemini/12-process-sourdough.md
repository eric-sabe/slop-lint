Baking sourdough bread from scratch is part science, part art, and entirely magical. Unlike commercial bread made with instant yeast, sourdough relies on a lively colony of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria. This ancient method yields a loaf with a crackly crust, an airy crumb, and a distinct, tangy flavor. Here is a comprehensive guide to baking your first loaf.

**The Prerequisite: The Starter**
Before baking, you need an active sourdough starter—a fermented mixture of flour and water. If starting from absolute scratch, it takes about 7 to 10 days of daily feeding to build a strong starter. For this recipe, your starter must be bubbly, doubled in size, and pass the "float test" (a small spoonful of it should easily float in a glass of water). 

**The Ingredients and Tools**
You need only four humble ingredients: 100 grams of active starter, 350 grams of warm water, 500 grams of bread flour (higher protein helps with gluten development), and 10 grams of fine sea salt. You will also need a mixing bowl, a proofing basket (banneton), a razor blade (lame), and a cast-iron Dutch oven.

**Step 1: Mixing and Autolyse**
Begin by dissolving your active starter into the warm water. Once the mixture is milky, add the bread flour. Mix with your hands until a shaggy, dry dough forms and no dry pockets of flour remain. Do not add the salt just yet. Cover the bowl with a damp towel and let it rest for 45 minutes. This resting period, called the *autolyse*, allows the flour to fully hydrate and jumpstarts the gluten development without any kneading.

**Step 2: Adding Salt and Bulk Fermentation**
Sprinkle the salt over the dough. Wet your hands to prevent sticking, and pinch the salt into the dough, folding it over itself until it is fully incorporated. 

Now begins the bulk fermentation, which takes 4 to 6 hours depending on your kitchen's temperature. Instead of traditional kneading, you will perform a series of "stretch and folds" to build structure. Every 30 minutes for the first two hours, grab one side of the dough, stretch it up toward the ceiling, and fold it over the center. Rotate the bowl and repeat four times until you have gone full circle. After this stretching phase, let the dough rest undisturbed until it is puffy, jiggly, and increased in volume by about 50 percent.

**Step 3: Shaping**
Turn the fermented dough out onto a lightly floured counter. To pre-shape, gently pull the edges into the center to form a loose round. Let it rest on the counter for 20 minutes. 

For the final shape, flip the dough over. Fold the bottom up, the sides in, and roll the top down until you have a tight package. Flip it seam-side down. Using your hands, cup the dough and drag it toward you, using the counter's friction to pull the top surface tight. Place the dough seam-side up into a generously floured proofing basket.

**Step 4: The Cold Proof**
Cover the basket with a damp towel or a plastic bag and place it in the refrigerator for 12 to 15 hours. This cold retard significantly slows down fermentation, deepening that signature sourdough flavor and making the dough much firmer and easier to score.

**Step 5: Scoring and Baking**
Place your Dutch oven (with the lid on) inside your oven and preheat to 500°F (260°C) for one hour. 

Invert your proofing basket to release the cold dough onto a piece of parchment paper. Using your lame or a very sharp knife, make a firm, half-inch-deep slash across the top of the dough. This allows the bread to expand gracefully in the heat.

Carefully use the parchment paper as a sling to lower the dough into the scorching hot Dutch oven. Cover with the lid, reduce the oven temperature to 450°F (230°C), and bake for 20 minutes. The trapped steam inside the pot is the secret to a blistered, professional crust. 

Remove the lid. The bread should have puffed up significantly (known as "oven spring"). Continue baking uncovered for 20 to 25 minutes until the crust is a deep, dark, caramelized brown.

**Step 6: The Hardest Part—Waiting**
Transfer the baked loaf to a wire cooling rack. Resist the urge to cut into it immediately! The bread is still cooking on the inside, and slicing too early will result in a gummy texture. Wait at least two hours before slicing, then enjoy your masterpiece with plenty of good butter.
