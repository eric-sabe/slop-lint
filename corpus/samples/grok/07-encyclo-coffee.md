History of Coffee

Coffee is a brewed beverage prepared from the roasted seeds of plants in the genus *Coffea*, primarily *Coffea arabica* and *Coffea canephora*. Its documented history begins in the Ethiopian highlands and extends through successive waves of cultivation, trade, and cultural adoption that transformed it from a regional stimulant into one of the world’s most widely consumed commodities.

Ethiopian oral tradition credits the discovery of coffee’s stimulating properties to a goatherd named Kaldi, who reportedly observed his animals becoming unusually energetic after consuming bright red berries from a wild shrub. While the precise date remains uncertain, botanical and linguistic evidence places the plant’s early use in the region of Kaffa (modern-day Ethiopia) by the ninth century or earlier. Sufi monks in Yemen are credited with the first systematic cultivation and roasting of the beans, employing the drink to sustain wakefulness during nocturnal prayers. By the late fifteenth century, coffee had reached the port of Mocha, from which it took one of its enduring names, and had spread across the Arabian Peninsula.

The beverage entered the Ottoman Empire in the early sixteenth century. Coffeehouses opened in Mecca, Cairo, and Constantinople (Istanbul), becoming centers of social and intellectual exchange. Ottoman authorities periodically attempted to suppress these establishments, viewing them as sites of political discussion, yet coffee consumption continued to grow. European travelers and merchants encountered the drink in Levantine ports; the first coffeehouse in Venice opened in 1645, followed by establishments in Oxford (1650) and London (1652). In Paris, cafés appeared in the 1670s. Initial European responses ranged from papal scrutiny—Pope Clement VIII is said to have tasted and approved the beverage—to medical debates over its effects on health.

Supply remained limited to Yemeni and Ethiopian sources until the late seventeenth century, when the Dutch succeeded in transplanting seedlings to their colonies. Java became a major producer by the 1710s. French and British colonial efforts subsequently introduced the plant to the Caribbean and South America. Gabriel de Clieu is traditionally credited with bringing a viable cutting to Martinique in 1720; from there, cultivation spread to Haiti, Jamaica, and, crucially, Brazil. By the early nineteenth century, Brazil had emerged as the dominant global supplier, its vast plantations fueled by enslaved and later immigrant labor. Colombia, Central America, and parts of Africa followed, creating the broad geographic pattern of production that persists today.

The nineteenth century witnessed both expansion and crisis. Coffee rust (*Hemileia vastatrix*) devastated plantations in Ceylon and parts of India after 1860, shifting production emphasis toward Latin America. Industrial roasting and vacuum packaging improved shelf life, while the rise of railroads and steamships lowered transport costs. The twentieth century brought further transformation: instant coffee, developed commercially in the 1930s, gained prominence during World War II. Postwar decolonization altered trade relations, prompting the formation of the International Coffee Agreement (1962) to stabilize prices. Brazil’s periodic frosts and the emergence of new producers such as Vietnam in the 1990s periodically reshaped market dynamics.

In the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, differentiation between commodity-grade and specialty coffee became pronounced. Direct-trade models, certification programs, and single-origin marketing reflected consumer interest in quality and provenance. Global production now exceeds 10 million metric tons annually, with consumption centered in Europe, North America, and increasingly Asia. Throughout its history, coffee has functioned simultaneously as an agricultural commodity, a social lubricant, and a catalyst for economic and political change across continents.
