Sourdough bread stands out for its tangy flavor, chewy crumb, and crisp crust, all achieved through natural fermentation rather than commercial yeast. Baking it from scratch begins with cultivating a wild yeast starter and proceeds through mixing, fermentation, shaping, and baking. The process spans several days but requires minimal hands-on time. Below is a complete guide yielding one large loaf.

To create a sourdough starter, combine equal parts flour and water in a clean jar. On day one, mix 50 grams whole rye or all-purpose flour with 50 grams room-temperature filtered water. Stir vigorously, cover loosely with a lid or cloth, and rest at 70–75°F. Bubbles should appear within 24 hours. Each subsequent day, discard all but 50 grams of the mixture and feed it with 50 grams flour and 50 grams water. By day three or four, the starter typically doubles in volume within four to six hours and emits a pleasant, yeasty aroma. Continue daily feedings until it reliably rises and falls predictably, usually by day five to seven. Once active, refrigerate and feed weekly if not baking often. A mature starter can last indefinitely with proper care.

For the bread itself, gather 100 grams active starter, 350 grams water, 500 grams bread flour, and 10 grams salt. Begin with an autolyse: mix the flour and water until no dry flour remains, then rest 30 minutes. This hydrates the flour and develops gluten early. Next, add the starter and salt, mixing by hand until incorporated. Perform a series of stretch-and-folds every 30 minutes for the first two hours: reach under the dough, stretch a portion upward, and fold it over the center. Rotate the bowl and repeat four times per session. This builds strength without traditional kneading.

Bulk fermentation follows at room temperature for four to six hours total, until the dough increases 50–75 percent in volume and shows airy bubbles. Perform two to three additional sets of folds during the first half. The dough should feel supple and elastic. Turn it onto a lightly floured surface, shape into a round or oval by folding edges toward the center, then flip seam-side down and tension the surface with a bench scraper. Place seam-side up in a floured proofing basket or towel-lined bowl. Cover and refrigerate overnight for cold proofing, which develops flavor and makes scoring easier.

The next day, preheat a Dutch oven to 450°F for one hour. Turn the dough onto parchment, score the top with a sharp blade for controlled expansion, and transfer to the hot pot. Bake covered 20 minutes to trap steam, then uncovered 25–30 minutes until deep mahogany. Internal temperature should reach 205–210°F. Cool on a wire rack at least two hours before slicing.
