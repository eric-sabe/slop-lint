Sony WH-1000XM5 Wireless Noise Canceling Headphones Review

After spending the past six weeks with Sony’s flagship over-ear headphones, the WH-1000XM5, I can confidently say they represent the current pinnacle of wireless ANC listening for most people. Priced at $398, they sit at the premium end of the market, yet they justify the cost through incremental but meaningful upgrades over the already excellent XM4 model. Whether you commute daily, work from home, or simply want the best possible audio isolation on a plane, these headphones deliver a polished, feature-rich experience that few competitors match.

The first thing you notice is the redesign. Sony moved away from the hinged earcups of previous generations to a sleeker, more minimalist look. The headband is now a single piece of smooth leatherette rather than the segmented design of the XM4, which gives the headphones a premium, almost Apple-like feel. They weigh 250 grams—only 10 grams more than the previous model—yet feel lighter on the head because the clamping force has been redistributed. After four-hour listening sessions, I experienced almost no pressure points around the ears. The earcups themselves are deeper and wider, accommodating larger ears comfortably. The only minor complaint is that the new matte finish attracts fingerprints more readily than the glossy XM4, though a quick wipe restores the clean appearance.

Sound quality is where the XM5 shines brightest. Sony tuned these headphones with a slightly warmer, more refined signature than the XM4. Bass remains punchy and textured without overwhelming the mids, which now carry better clarity on vocals and acoustic instruments. Highs are crisp yet never harsh, even at higher volumes. I tested across genres: on Billie Eilish’s “bad guy,” the sub-bass hits with satisfying weight while the whispered vocals stay front and center. Classical pieces like Max Richter’s “On the Nature of Daylight” reveal excellent instrument separation and a convincing sense of space. Sony’s 360 Reality Audio and Hi-Res Wireless codecs (LDAC) are fully supported, and when paired with a compatible Android device, the difference in resolution is noticeable.

The real star remains the active noise canceling. Sony claims an 8% improvement over the XM4, and in practice it feels more substantial. On a recent flight, the low-frequency rumble of the engines was reduced to a faint hum, allowing me to listen at moderate volumes without fatigue. Office chatter and keyboard typing are also attenuated effectively, though sudden high-pitched sounds like a ringing phone still leak through slightly. Transparency mode is equally impressive; it lets in ambient sound naturally without the artificial “hiss” some competitors introduce. Switching between ANC and transparency is seamless via the touch controls or the excellent Sony Headphones Connect app.

Battery life is rated at 30 hours with ANC on and 40 hours with it off—figures that match real-world usage almost exactly. After a week of mixed use (two-hour commutes plus occasional Zoom calls), I only needed to charge once. A quick three-minute charge provides up to three hours of playback, which is genuinely useful. The headphones support multipoint Bluetooth, allowing seamless switching between my laptop and phone, and the new speak-to-chat feature automatically pauses music and enables transparency when it detects your voice.

Call quality has improved thanks to additional beamforming microphones and AI noise reduction. In a moderately noisy café, callers reported clear speech with minimal background interference. Wind noise remains the weak point outdoors, but that’s true of virtually every over-ear headset.

The app offers extensive customization: EQ presets, adaptive sound control that learns your locations, and 360 Reality Audio calibration. Firmware updates have already added minor refinements since launch, showing Sony’s commitment to ongoing support.

Criticisms are relatively minor. The lack of a 3.5 mm wired option (a cable is included but requires the headphones to be powered on) feels like an unnecessary step backward for some users. The touch controls, while responsive, can occasionally register accidental swipes when adjusting the fit. At $398, they’re expensive; if your budget is tighter, the XM4 remains an outstanding value at around $278.

Overall, the WH-1000XM5 are the headphones I now reach for daily. They combine class-leading noise cancellation, comfortable all-day wear, and refined sound in a package that feels thoughtfully engineered. If you value silence and sonic detail above all else and can stretch your budget, these are worth every penny. For everyone else, they set a high bar that competing models will struggle to clear in 2023.
