# The Town of Mercer's Hollow

Mercer's Hollow sits in a fold of land where two rivers meet, as if the earth itself had cupped its hands to hold the place steady against the rush of the wider world. You arrive by a single road that winds down from the ridge, and the first thing you notice is how the town reveals itself slowly—a church steeple, then rooftops, then the whole modest sprawl of it laid out along the water like laundry on a line.

The people of Mercer's Hollow have a particular way about them, shaped by generations of staying put. They are not unfriendly, but they are watchful. A stranger walking down Main Street will be greeted with a nod and a measured glance, the kind of look that takes inventory without seeming to. It is not suspicion exactly—more a quiet cataloging, a way of placing you in the order of things. By suppertime, half the town will know you've come, and by morning, the other half will have an opinion about why.

Consider Della Munroe, who has run the diner on the corner for thirty-one years. She knows everyone's order before they sit down and refuses to write any of it on a ticket, claiming her memory is "the only thing in this town that still works right." Della speaks her mind freely, dispensing advice along with the coffee, and she has comforted more grieving widows and scolded more wayward teenagers than the pastor and the principal combined. People complain about her bluntness, but they keep coming back, because underneath the sharp tongue is a woman who has never once let a hungry person leave without a meal, paying customer or not.

Then there is old Wendell Pryce, who lives at the edge of town in a house overrun with books and clocks. He was a schoolteacher once, and though he retired long ago, the children still drift to his porch on summer evenings to hear him tell stories about the town's founding, about the flood of '54, about the men who first built the mill. Wendell believes that a town without its stories is just a collection of buildings, and he has appointed himself the keeper of Mercer's Hollow's memory. The young people humor him at first and then, somewhere along the way, find themselves genuinely listening.

The character of the Hollow is one of stubborn endurance. The mill closed twenty years back, and by all rights the town should have hollowed out and died like so many others. But the people here have a kind of dogged loyalty to the place that defies economic sense. They patched together a living from small farms, from the hardware store and the auto shop, from the tourists who pass through on their way to the lake. They held bake sales to keep the library open. When the Hendersons' barn burned, the whole town showed up with hammers and lumber, and within a month a new one stood in its place. They do not speak much about this kindness; they simply enact it, as naturally as breathing.

Yet they are not saints, and Mercer's Hollow is no paradise. There are old grudges that have curdled over decades, families who haven't spoken since some forgotten slight three generations back. Gossip travels fast and rarely flatters. The town can be insular, slow to accept change, suspicious of anyone whose ideas come from too far away. A young person with ambition often finds the place too small, the expectations too fixed, and many leave for the cities and do not return. Those who stay carry a complicated love for the town—a love that knows its faults intimately and chooses it anyway.

What binds them, finally, is a shared understanding that they belong to something larger than themselves. They are the inheritors of a place, and they feel the weight of that inheritance even when they cannot name it. They tend the same gardens their grandparents tended. They bury their dead in the same hillside cemetery and visit those graves with flowers in spring.

Mercer's Hollow endures, not because it is remarkable, but because its people have decided, quietly and collectively, that it should.
