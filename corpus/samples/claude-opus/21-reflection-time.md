# The Quickening: How Time Changes as We Age

There is a peculiar paradox at the heart of human experience: the days can feel endless while the years vanish in an instant. Ask any child waiting for a birthday, and they will tell you that time crawls. Ask anyone past forty about the last decade, and they will likely shake their head in disbelief at how quickly it slipped away. We all live by the same clock, yet our experience of time bends and warps as we move through life.

## The Long Summers of Childhood

When we are young, time stretches luxuriously before us. A single summer can feel like an entire epoch, rich with adventure and possibility. There are scientific explanations for this. One theory suggests that our sense of time is tied to novelty. In childhood, nearly everything is new. Each day brings first experiences—first tastes, first friendships, first failures and triumphs. The brain, working hard to encode all this unfamiliar information, creates dense, detailed memories. When we look back, these crowded mental records make the period seem long, because so much happened within it.

There is also the matter of proportion. To a five-year-old, one year represents twenty percent of their entire existence. To a fifty-year-old, that same year is merely two percent of a life already lived. Each passing year becomes a smaller fraction of our accumulated experience, and so it feels correspondingly briefer. This "ratio theory," first proposed in the nineteenth century, offers an intuitive explanation for why birthdays seem to arrive faster and faster.

## The Acceleration of Adulthood

As we settle into adulthood, routine takes hold. We commute the same roads, perform familiar tasks, move through weeks that resemble the ones before. Novelty grows scarce. When little distinguishes one day from another, the brain has less to record, and time seems to compress. Weeks blur into months, and we find ourselves startled to realize that a season has passed, or that a child we knew as a toddler is suddenly halfway grown.

This compression can feel unsettling, even melancholic. We sense time slipping through our fingers and wonder where it went. Yet this experience is not inevitable. The same mechanisms that make time race can be gently reversed. When we travel to an unfamiliar place, take up a new skill, or break our patterns, time seems to expand again. A week of vacation in a foreign country can feel longer in memory than a month of ordinary routine. Novelty, it turns out, is one of the few tools we have for slowing the perceived rush of years.

## Time and Mortality

There is another dimension to how aging shapes our relationship with time: our growing awareness of its limits. In youth, time feels infinite, an inexhaustible resource we can afford to squander. We make plans for distant futures with casual confidence. But as we age, the horizon draws closer. We become conscious that our days are numbered, and this awareness changes how we value them.

Psychologist Laura Carstensen's research on "socioemotional selectivity" reveals something hopeful here. As people perceive their time growing shorter, they often shift their priorities toward what matters most—deeper relationships, meaningful experiences, emotional richness over mere accumulation. Older adults frequently report greater contentment, savoring the present rather than chasing the future. The shrinking of time, paradoxically, can deepen our appreciation of it.

## Living Within the Paradox

Perhaps the lesson in all this is that our experience of time is not fixed but malleable, shaped by attention, novelty, and meaning. We cannot stop the clock, nor can we recover the elastic summers of childhood. But we can influence how full our time feels. By staying curious, by seeking new experiences, by paying genuine attention to the moments we inhabit, we can resist the blur of routine.

To age is to watch time accelerate, but it is also to understand it more honestly. The child who waits impatiently for tomorrow and the elder who treasures today are both responding to the same fundamental truth: time is the medium in which we live our entire lives. How we experience it depends, in no small part, on how fully we choose to be present within it.
