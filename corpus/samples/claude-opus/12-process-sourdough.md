# How to Bake Sourdough Bread from Scratch

Baking sourdough bread is a rewarding journey that combines science, patience, and a little bit of art. Unlike conventional bread that uses commercial yeast, sourdough relies on a natural fermentation process driven by wild yeast and beneficial bacteria. Here's a complete guide to get you from scratch to a beautiful, crusty loaf.

## Step 1: Create Your Sourdough Starter

Everything begins with the starter—a living culture of flour and water that captures wild yeast from the environment. To make one, combine 50 grams of whole wheat flour with 50 grams of room-temperature water in a clean jar. Stir well, cover loosely, and leave it at room temperature.

Each day for the next five to seven days, "feed" your starter by discarding about half of it and adding 50 grams of fresh flour and 50 grams of water. Within a few days, you'll notice bubbles forming and a tangy, slightly sour smell developing. When your starter doubles in size within four to six hours after feeding and looks bubbly and active, it's ready to bake with. This process typically takes about a week, though it can vary based on temperature and conditions.

## Step 2: Gather Your Ingredients

For a basic sourdough loaf, you'll need:

- 100 grams active sourdough starter
- 375 grams water (room temperature)
- 500 grams bread flour
- 10 grams salt

That's it—just four ingredients. The simplicity is part of sourdough's beauty.

## Step 3: Mix and Autolyse

In a large bowl, combine the active starter with the water and stir to dissolve. Add the flour and mix until no dry patches remain. The dough will look shaggy and rough. Cover the bowl and let it rest for 30 to 60 minutes. This resting period, called the **autolyse**, allows the flour to fully hydrate and begins gluten development, making the dough easier to work with later.

After the autolyse, add the salt and mix it thoroughly into the dough by squeezing and folding.

## Step 4: Bulk Fermentation and Stretch-and-Folds

Now begins the bulk fermentation, which usually lasts four to six hours at room temperature. During the first couple of hours, you'll perform a series of "stretch-and-folds" to build strength in the dough. Every 30 minutes, wet your hands, grab one side of the dough, stretch it upward, and fold it over itself. Rotate the bowl and repeat on all four sides. Do this four times total over two hours.

After the folds, let the dough rest undisturbed for the remainder of the fermentation. The dough is ready when it has risen by about 50 percent, looks puffy, and shows some bubbles on the surface.

## Step 5: Shape the Dough

Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Gently shape it into a round by folding the edges into the center and flipping it seam-side down. Use your hands or a bench scraper to drag the dough across the surface, creating tension on the surface. Let it rest for 20 minutes, then do a final shaping.

Place the shaped dough seam-side up into a floured banneton (proofing basket) or a bowl lined with a floured tea towel.

## Step 6: Final Proof

Cover the dough and let it proof. You have two options: proof at room temperature for two to four hours, or refrigerate it overnight (8 to 12 hours). The cold "retard" method develops deeper flavor and makes scoring easier, so many bakers prefer it.

## Step 7: Bake

Preheat your oven to 475°F (245°C) with a Dutch oven inside for at least 30 minutes. The Dutch oven traps steam, which is essential for a crisp crust.

Carefully turn your dough out onto parchment paper. Using a sharp blade or razor, score the top with a quick, confident slash about a quarter-inch deep. This controls how the bread expands.

Lower the dough into the hot Dutch oven, cover, and bake for 20 minutes. Then remove the lid and bake for another 20 to 25 minutes until the crust is deeply golden brown.

## Step 8: Cool and Enjoy

Transfer the bread to a wire rack and resist the temptation to cut it immediately. Let it cool for at least one hour so the interior can finish setting. Then slice, and savor your homemade sourdough—crackling crust, tangy flavor, and all.
